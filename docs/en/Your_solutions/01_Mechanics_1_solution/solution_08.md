### Theory: Uniform Circular Motion

When an object moves in a circle at a constant speed, it is in **Uniform Circular Motion**. Even though the speed is constant, the velocity is always changing because the *direction* of the motion is constantly changing. 

According to Newton's laws, a change in velocity means there is an acceleration. In circular motion, this acceleration always points toward the center of the circular path. This is called **centripetal acceleration** (from Latin, meaning "center-seeking").

A person standing on the Earth's equator travels in a massive circle once every day as the Earth rotates on its axis. Therefore, that person experiences a small centripetal acceleration pointing toward the center of the Earth.

#### Basic Formulas

To solve this, we use the kinematics of circular motion:

1.  **Period (T)**: The time it takes to complete one full revolution.
2.  **Angular Velocity (Пү)**: The rate at which the angle changes.
    `Пү = 2ПҖ / T`
3.  **Tangential Velocity (v)**: The linear speed of the object along the edge of the circle.
    `v = 2ПҖR / T = ПүR`
4.  **Centripetal Acceleration (a_c)**: The acceleration directed toward the center.
    `a_c = vВІ / R = ПүВІR`

### Step-by-Step Solution

#### Step 1: Identify and convert the given data into SI units
In physics, it is crucial to work in standard SI units: meters (m) for distance and seconds (s) for time.

* **Radius of the Earth (R)**: 
    The problem gives R = 6378 km. We need to convert this to meters.
    `R = 6378 km * 1000 m/km = 6,378,000 m = 6.378 Г— 10вҒ¶ m`

* **Period of Rotation (T)**:
    The Earth rotates once per day. A standard solar day is 24 hours. Let's convert this to seconds. *(Note: for higher precision, physicists sometimes use a "sidereal day" which is about 23 hours and 56 minutes, but a 24-hour period is standard for this type of problem).*
    `T = 24 hours * 60 minutes/hour * 60 seconds/minute`
    `T = 86,400 s`

#### Step 2: Calculate the angular velocity (Пү)
Angular velocity tells us how many radians the Earth rotates through per second.

`Пү = 2ПҖ / T`

Substituting our value for T:

`Пү = (2 * 3.14159) / 86400 s`
`Пү вүҲ 7.272 Г— 10вҒ»вҒө rad/s`

#### Step 3: Calculate the centripetal acceleration (a_c)
Now we can use the formula for centripetal acceleration that relates to angular velocity and radius.

`a_c = ПүВІR`

Substituting our known values:

`a_c = (7.272 Г— 10вҒ»вҒө rad/s)ВІ * (6.378 Г— 10вҒ¶ m)`

First, square the angular velocity:

`(7.272 Г— 10вҒ»вҒө)ВІ вүҲ 5.288 Г— 10вҒ»вҒ№ radВІ/sВІ`

Next, multiply by the radius:

`a_c вүҲ (5.288 Г— 10вҒ»вҒ№) * (6.378 Г— 10вҒ¶)`
`a_c вүҲ 0.0337 m/sВІ`

---

### Final Answer
The centripetal acceleration of a person standing on the Earth's equator is approximately **0.0337 m/sВІ**.

> **Physics Insight:** Notice how small this value is compared to the acceleration due to Earth's gravity (g вүҲ 9.81 m/sВІ). The centripetal acceleration required to keep you moving in a circle with the Earth subtracts slightly from the gravitational pull you feel. This is why you actually weigh a tiny bit less at the equator than you do at the poles!
