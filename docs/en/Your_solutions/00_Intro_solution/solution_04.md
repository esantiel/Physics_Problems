# 4. Rearranging Formulas

## Problem

The formula for the **period of a simple pendulum** is

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

where

- $T$ = period of oscillation (seconds)
- $L$ = length of the pendulum (meters)
- $g$ = acceleration due to gravity ($m/s^2$)

**Goal:**  
Rearrange the equation to obtain a formula for **$g$**.

---

# 1️⃣ Basic Theory

A **simple pendulum** swings back and forth with a repeating motion called **periodic motion**.

The **period** $T$ is the time required for **one complete oscillation**.

For small oscillations, the period is given by

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

This formula shows:

- A **longer pendulum** increases the period.
- **Stronger gravity** decreases the period.

Our task is to **rearrange this equation so that $g$ is the subject**.

---

# 2️⃣ Step-by-Step Rearrangement

We start with the original formula.

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

---

## Step 1: Divide Both Sides by $2\pi$

To isolate the square root term, divide both sides by $2\pi$.

$$
\frac{T}{2\pi} = \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

Now the square root is isolated.

---

## Step 2: Square Both Sides

To remove the square root, square both sides of the equation.

$$
\left(\frac{T}{2\pi}\right)^2 = \frac{L}{g}
$$

Expand the square:

$$
\frac{T^2}{4\pi^2} = \frac{L}{g}
$$

---

## Step 3: Multiply Both Sides by $g$

We now want to eliminate $g$ from the denominator.

$$
g \cdot \frac{T^2}{4\pi^2} = L
$$

---

## Step 4: Solve for $g$

Divide both sides by $\frac{T^2}{4\pi^2}$.

$$
g = \frac{L}{\frac{T^2}{4\pi^2}}
$$

---

## Step 5: Simplify the Fraction

Dividing by a fraction is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal.

$$
g = L \cdot \frac{4\pi^2}{T^2}
$$

Rearranging gives the final form:

$$
g = \frac{4\pi^2 L}{T^2}
$$

---

# ✅ Final Result

The acceleration due to gravity is

$$
g = \frac{4\pi^2 L}{T^2}
$$

---

# 📌 Interpretation

This formula allows us to **measure gravity experimentally**.

If we measure:

- the **length of the pendulum** $L$
- the **period of oscillation** $T$

we can compute the **local gravitational acceleration $g$**.

---

# 🎯 Key Idea

The period of a pendulum depends on:

- the **length of the pendulum**
- the **gravitational acceleration**

Rearranging the formula gives a way to **calculate gravity from experimental measurements**.
